Wei Li · LTRG — Method Distillation

https://arxiv.org/abs/1011.0155

Method-usage tricks of the Linearized Tensor Renormalization Group (LTRG) as practiced by Wei Li, distilled from the originating paper (Li et al., PRL 2010).

Scope, tooling & cost

Cases, tooling, and limits are surveyed across the LTRG family (LTRG → XTRG → tanTRG); the tricks below derive from the 2010 LTRG paper.

ItemContentEvidence
What it doesFinite-temperature thermodynamics — free energy, internal energy, specific heat — of one- and two-dimensional quantum lattice models, reaching the thermodynamic limit (an effectively infinite system) in one dimension.abstract; Eq.(2)–(3)
Typical casesBenchmarked on one-dimensional XY and Heisenberg chains (this paper); the successors XTRG and tanTRG extend it to two-dimensional cylinders of width 8–10 (square-, triangular-, and kagome-lattice magnets) and to the two-dimensional Hubbard model at temperatures of a few percent of the hopping energy.arXiv:1011.0155, 1801.00142, 2212.11973
Best-fitTwo-dimensional systems at finite temperature for which quantum Monte Carlo is obstructed by the sign problem (negative sampling weights).closing summary; honeycomb test
BoundaryIn two dimensions the accessible cylinder width (≈8–10) is set by the bond dimension Dc rather than the total site count, and quantitative validation is restricted to the sign-problem-free regime.Fig. 5; XTRG / tanTRG
Software & toolsNo general open-source LTRG implementation exists: LTRG and its successors (XTRG, tanTRG) are distributed only in the authors' QMagen package (MATLAB), and are otherwise implemented in-house on a tensor / SVD library. The controlling parameters are intrinsic to LTRG — the bond dimension Dc (retained states) and the imaginary-time step.QMagen; method papers
ImplementationA one-dimensional core is ≈300–800 lines on a tensor / SVD library (≈1.5–3k for full XTRG / tanTRG). The principal difficulties are maintaining a stable truncation toward low temperature and implementing symmetry-resolved tensor contractions.method papers
Compute cost (CPU core-hours)Each imaginary-time step scales as O(D6Dc3) — D = local state dimension (2 for spin-½), Dc = bond dimension. Total cost ≈ this × number of imaginary-time steps × number of temperature points. Absolute core-hours are not reported and remain an interview target.Fig. 2 caption

Method-usage tricks

Techniques for executing the method reliably.

TrickWhat it isWhy it mattersEvidence
Linear decimationThe transfer-matrix network is decimated one row per imaginary-time step, discarding only Dc singular values per step — versus the older coarse-graining renormalization (TRG), which discards substantially more.Origin of LTRG's accuracy and scalability over TRG.after Eq.(6); Fig. 2 caption
Normalization-factor accumulationSingular values are rescaled by their largest element at each step to prevent divergence of the imaginary-time evolution; the free energy is reconstructed from the accumulated normalization factors.The discarded norms encode the free energy.free-energy paragraph (Fig. 3)
Error-source attribution by regimeIndependent variation of the imaginary-time step and the bond dimension Dc isolates the error source: at high temperature it saturates in Dc (time-discretization–limited); at low temperature it decreases with Dc (truncation-limited).Identifies which parameter limits accuracy in each regime.Fig. 4
Thermodynamic limit via pairwise contractionPairwise contraction of 2p matrices over p steps yields 2p sites — here 2100, effectively the thermodynamic limit — at a cost growing only with p.Removes finite-size error at negligible cost in one dimension.free-energy paragraph

Physical insights underlying the calculation.

TrickWhat it isWhy it mattersEvidence
Quantum-to-(D+1) classical mappingA Trotter–Suzuki decomposition recasts the D-dimensional quantum model as a classical tensor network in one higher dimension; the thermal density operator becomes a matrix product operator (a one-dimensional tensor chain).Reduces thermodynamics to a tensor-network contraction.intro; Eq.(2)–(3)
Impurity-tensor observablesThe internal energy is read from a local impurity tensor inserted in the network, cross-validated against the temperature derivative of the free energy.A measurement intrinsic to the network, not finite differencing.internal-energy paragraph
